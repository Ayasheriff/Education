“Curriculum”

is a term that refers to the courses of study offered or planned by an educational institution or program. It can also include the learning experiences, skills, attitudes, and values that pupils are expected to acquire from schooling. Curriculum can be organized in different ways, such as by subject, grade, or sequence, and can vary across classrooms, schools, districts, states, or countries.

“Scientific subjects rely solely on memorization and indoctrination.”

Rote learning is a memorization technique based on repetition. The method rests on the premise that the recall of repeated material becomes faster the more one repeats it. Some of the alternatives to rote learning include meaningful learning, associative learning, spaced repetition and active learning. Rote learning is widely used in the mastery of foundational knowledge. Examples of school topics where rote learning is frequently used include phonics in reading, the periodic table in chemistry, multiplication tables in mathematics, anatomy in medicine, cases or statutes in law, basic formulas in any science, etc. By definition, rote learning eschews comprehension, so by itself it is an ineffective tool in mastering any complex subject at an advanced level. For instance, one illustration of rote learning can be observed in preparing quickly for exams, a technique which may be colloquially referred to as “cramming”.

"The financial return is very low, which makes the teacher resort to private lessons."

One issue that can arise when teachers do not receive adequate financial compensation for their work is that they may feel undervalued and demotivated. This can result in teachers seeking additional sources of income, such as private tutoring, to supplement their income. This can lead to teachers being overworked and unable to devote their full attention and energy to their students in the classroom.

Lets solve this

One way to address this issue is to ensure that teachers receive fair and adequate compensation for their work. This can involve increasing teacher salaries and providing additional benefits such as health insurance, retirement plans, and paid time off. Schools can also provide opportunities for career advancement and professional growth, which can help teachers feel valued and motivated. In addition to improving teacher compensation, schools can also work to reduce the workload of teachers by providing them with adequate support and resources. This can involve hiring additional staff to assist with administrative tasks, providing teachers with planning time, and reducing class sizes to allow teachers to provide more individualized attention to their students.
